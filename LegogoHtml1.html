Chairs of the Cosmos Acronym: COTC

Created: Wednesday -- 15 Oct 2025 ‚Ä¢ 10:30 AM (Philippines time) -- event: Bondoc tossed a folded paper at Ross

About

In this playful classroom role‚Äëplaying game, each chair or group of chairs is treated as a fully fledged planet and the students are its alien inhabitants. Every object on or under a chair -- books, crumbs, pencils, even flies and microbes -- becomes part of that planet's ecosystem. The teacher's desk functions as a star or a black hole. Players exchange paper transmissions (notes) containing questions, dares, drawings, or games to interact across the classroom galaxy.

Goal

Interact with other aliens (students) and their planets using funny, creative paper transmissions. The purpose is not competition but to keep the galaxy alive, chaotic, and hilarious through constant interplanetary communication.

Rules

  1. Every chair (or group of chairs) is a planet.
  2. The person sitting on it is the King/Almighty of that planet.
  3. Anything on or under your desk/chair (books, crumbs, flies, etc.) is part of your planet.
  4. The teacher's desk is a star or black hole.
    A chair becomes a star if someone writes STAR on its desk.
  5. Communicate using paper transmissions -- write dares, jokes, drawings, or random alien messages and toss them to other planets.
  6. You may form alliances, trade items, or declare silly wars.
  7. Sometimes it's fine to say "classroom," "chair," or "desk" if it fits the joke or improves the fun.
  8. Never be boring -- make your planet sound weird, funny, or mysterious.
  9. There's no required winner -- the aim is to preserve galactic classroom chaos!

How to interact with other aliens

Send a paper transmission (or gently toss a paper) to another planet and let them read it. Transmissions can be questions, dares, drawings, or short games. Players are encouraged to roleplay as their planet's inhabitants and speak as aliens -- not as students -- describing landscapes, cultures, and odd events.

Victory & conflicts (optional)

Although the game is primarily cooperative and chaotic, an interplanetary war can be declared for fun. If a war eliminates all other planets, the last planet standing is the winner. Military units are playful objects such as origami, toy soldiers, rocks, cardboard shields, and similar harmless props.

Black hole rule

To convert a chair into a black hole, write BH[amount of black holes in the classroom] on the desk. If the written amount is incorrect, the attempt fails and the chair becomes a star instead -- the player must cross out the incorrect BH[...] and replace it with STAR.

Origin note

This version was recorded on Wednesday, 15 Oct 2025 at ~9:30 AM (Philippines time) when Bondoc (a classmate) threw an imperfectly folded paper at Ross. The note referenced earlier events and became part of the COTC lore.

Chitinozoa of Volsconia

Overview

On the oceanic world of Volsconia, microscopic organic capsules known as chitinozoa once floated through the planet's ancient seas. These mysterious organisms are preserved in fossilized form within Volsconian sediment layers dating back over 450 million planetary cycles. Their biological function remains a subject of debate among xenopaleontologists -- some interpret them as the egg cases of early planktonic creatures, while others propose they were independent microorganisms adapted to drifting in the nutrient-rich currents of Volsconia's primordial oceans.

Structure and Composition

Each chitinozoan vessel is composed of a chitin-like biopolymer, remarkably resistant to decay. Many specimens display intricate ornamentation, spines, and neck-like extensions suggesting both mechanical and sensory adaptations. The image above shows a damaged specimen, its outer wall torn open to reveal tentacle-like projections that may have been involved in feeding or attachment.

Scientific Significance

The distribution of chitinozoan fossils across Volsconia's strata helps researchers reconstruct the planet's ancient environments and climate shifts. Because their morphologies changed rapidly through time, chitinozoa serve as key biostratigraphic markers, allowing scientists to date sedimentary layers with remarkable precision.

Legacy on Volsconia

Though extinct for eons, chitinozoa remain vital to our understanding of life's early evolution on Volsconia. Their resilience and diversity offer a glimpse into a time when microscopic life dominated the seas, laying the foundation for the complex biospheres that would eventually flourish across the planet.

Diplakrida

Class -- Diplakrida  ‚Ä¢  Phylum Gademichordata  ‚Ä¢  Native: Volsconia

Overview

Diplakrida is a class of colonial, sessile filter-feeders endemic to the moon Volsconia. Members of this class -- called Diplakrids -- form branching colonies composed of numerous zooids within chitinous tubes. They share strong morphological and developmental affinities with other Gadeseexthians and a notable resemblance to Rhabdopleurans (historical graptolite relatives).

Taxonomic snapshot
  • Domain: Gadeseexthia
  • Phylum: Gademichordata
  • Class: Diplakrida

Anatomy & Physiology

Diplakrids are built from repeating zooids arranged along branching stolons. Each zooid bears a lophophore-like feeding crown used to capture suspended particulate food. Internally, they display a dorsal nerve strand and a supportive notochord-like rod -- features shared with other Gademichordates. Their tubes are secreted from anterior glands and reinforced in some species with silicate fibers inherited from agmatan-like ancestry.

Genetic affinities

The class shows the following approximate genetic/endophenotypic composition:

  • ~80% shared with other Gadeseexthians
  • ~10% similarity to Graptolites/Rhabdopleurans (colony architecture)
  • ~5% Agmatan influence (mineralized fiber traits)
  • ~2.5% Sea urchin-like deuterostome genes
  • ~2.5% Mycoplasmakaryote-derived metabolic gene transfers

Ecology

Colonies typically anchor to rocky substrates or metallic outcrops in shallow seas, creating dense structural habitats. Diplakrids filter large volumes of water and are central to Volsconia's benthic-pelagic nutrient cycling. Some species synchronize bioluminescent pulses for in-colony communication and possible mate attraction.

Reproduction & Life Cycle

Diplakrids reproduce asexually by budding to expand colonies and sexually via synchronized gamete release tied to tidal and lunar cues. Planktonic larvae disperse briefly before settling to found new colonies.

Significance

Diplakrida exemplifies convergent evolution toward colonial filter-feeding body plans in a Gadeseexthian context, bridging morphological traits seen in extinct graptolites and living hemichordate-like relatives.
